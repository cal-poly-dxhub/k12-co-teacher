Subject Area: English Language Arts

Objective/Purpose:
(The student expectations must be clearly defined and in line with the district/state standards. For the student's benefit, explain what students will be able to do by the end of the lesson and why these objectives are important to accomplish.)
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
• Identify and analyze character traits using direct and indirect characterization techniques
• Support character analysis with specific textual evidence from the story
• Explain how character traits influence plot development and character relationships

This skill is essential for developing critical reading comprehension and will help students become more thoughtful readers who can understand deeper meanings in literature. Character analysis is a foundational skill that appears on state assessments and supports students' ability to make connections between literature and real life.
Materials/Resources/Equipment Needed:
• Copies of "Thank You, M'am" by Langston Hughes
• Character analysis graphic organizer handouts
• Chart paper and markers
• Sticky notes
• Document camera/projector
• Whiteboard and markers
• Character trait word bank reference sheet
Anticipatory Set:
(Before you dig into the meat of your lesson's instruction, set the stage for your students by tapping into their prior knowledge and giving the objectives a context. What activities will you use to focus students on the lesson for the day?)
Begin with a "People Watching" activity: Show students a photograph of two people interacting (without context). Ask them to write on sticky notes what they think each person is like based on their appearance, body language, and facial expressions. Have students share their observations and discuss how we make judgments about people's personalities. Connect this to how authors reveal character traits in stories—sometimes directly through description, and sometimes indirectly through actions, dialogue, and thoughts. Introduce today's learning objective and explain that we'll be character detectives analyzing Mrs. Luella Bates Washington Jones from "Thank You, M'am."

Direct Instruction (15 minutes):
• Define direct vs. indirect characterization using examples from familiar characters
• Explain the STEAL method: Speech, Thoughts, Effects on others, Actions, Looks
• Show how to find textual evidence and make inferences about character traits

Guided Reading (20 minutes):
• Read "Thank You, M'am" aloud as a class, stopping at key moments
• Think-aloud modeling: demonstrate how to identify character traits using STEAL
• Students follow along and annotate their copies, marking evidence of Mrs. Jones's character

Interactive Discussion (10 minutes):
• Facilitate class discussion about Mrs. Jones's character traits
• Record student responses on chart paper, requiring textual evidence for each trait identified
Model:
(If you will be demonstrating the skill or competence, how will this be done?)
Using the first few paragraphs of "Thank You, M'am," I will model the character analysis process on the document camera. I'll demonstrate how to:
• Identify Mrs. Jones's physical description (direct characterization)
• Analyze her actions when Roger tries to steal her purse (indirect characterization)
• Make inferences about her character traits based on her dialogue: "Pick up my pocketbook, boy, and give it here"
• Record findings on the graphic organizer, showing how to cite page numbers and use quotation marks
• Think aloud my reasoning: "When Mrs. Jones says this, it shows she's authoritative and confident because..."
Check for Understanding:
(Identify strategies to be used to determine if students have learned the objective.)
• Exit ticket: Students identify one character trait of Mrs. Jones and provide textual evidence
• Thumbs up/thumbs down check during instruction
• Random calling on students to explain their thinking
• Observation of student annotations during guided reading
• Quick pair-share: "Turn and tell your partner one thing Mrs. Jones does that reveals her personality"
• Review completed graphic organizers during guided practice
Guided Practice:
(Under your supervision, students are given the chance to practice and apply the skills you taught them through the instructional delivery.)
Partner Work (15 minutes):
Students work in pairs to complete their character analysis graphic organizers for Mrs. Jones. They must:
• Find at least 3 examples of direct characterization
• Find at least 3 examples of indirect characterization using STEAL
• Write the character trait and supporting evidence for each example

Teacher circulates, providing feedback and asking guiding questions:
• "What does this action tell us about Mrs. Jones?"
• "Can you find the exact words from the text that support this?"
• "How does this trait affect her relationship with Roger?"

Pairs share one finding with the class before moving to closure.
Closure:
(What method of review and evaluation will be used to complete the lesson? How will you wrap up the lesson by giving the lesson concepts further meaning for your students?)
Gallery Walk and Reflection (10 minutes):
• Post completed graphic organizers around the room
• Students do a quick gallery walk to see different interpretations
• Return to seats for final discussion: "How do Mrs. Jones's character traits make this story meaningful?"
• Connect to real life: "How can understanding people's character traits help us in our own relationships?"
• Preview tomorrow's lesson: "Tomorrow we'll analyze Roger's character and compare how both characters change throughout the story"
• Remind students that character analysis skills will help them in upcoming assessments and future reading
Independent Practice:
(List the assignment(s) that will be given to the students to ensure they have mastered the skill without teacher guidance.)
Homework Assignment:
Students will read a short story of their choice from their independent reading book (or provided options) and complete a character analysis graphic organizer for the main character. Requirements:
• Identify 2 examples of direct characterization
• Identify 3 examples of indirect characterization using STEAL
• Write a paragraph explaining how the character's traits influence the story's events
• Include proper citations with page numbers

Assessment: This assignment will be collected and graded using the character analysis rubric. Students who need additional support can choose from pre-selected shorter texts with guided questions.
